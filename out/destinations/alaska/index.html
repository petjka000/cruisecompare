About Alaska

Alaska offers one of the world's most dramatic cruising experiences, where towering glaciers calve into icy waters and bald eagles soar above ancient rainforests. The Inside Passage—a protected network of islands, fjords, and channels stretching from Washington state to Alaska—provides the classic route, while voyages to Glacier Bay National Park deliverfront-row seats to advancing glaciers and abundant wildlife including whales, bears, and mountain goats. Alaska cruises attract nature lovers, adventure seekers, and those seeking a dramatically different cruising environment compared to Caribbean routes. Whether departing from Seattle, Vancouver, or sailing one-way between ports, you'll encounter a landscape of superlatives: North America's tallest peaks, deepest fjords, and most pristine wilderness. The state's small frontier towns—Juneau, Skagway, Ketchikan—offer authentic Alaskan character with Gold Rush history, Native Alaskan culture, and seafood straight from the Pacific.

The Alaska cruise season runs from May to September, with daylight hours varying dramatically across the season. In late June, you experience nearly 19 hours of daylight in the northern ports, creating the famous Midnight Sun phenomenon. By September, nights lengthen and some attractions begin closing for the season. Most cruise ships sailing Alaska are mid-sized vessels designed to navigate the Inside Passage's narrow channels, with many featuring balcony cabins specifically marketed for glacier viewing. Onboard programming often includes naturalist lectures, wildlife identification guides, and Alaska-themed dining featuring local seafood like wild-caught salmon and king crab. A land extension to Denali National Park, reachable via the Alaska Railroad from Whittier or Anchorage, pairs perfectly with any cruise itinerary for a complete Alaskan experience.

What distinguishes Alaska from other cruise destinations is the constant interplay between civilization and wilderness. In Skagway, you can board a historic locomotive on the White Pass and Yukon Route Railway, climbing 2,865 feet through tunnels and over trestle bridges once used by Gold Rush prospectors. In Icy Strait Point, native Hoonah Tlingit cultural experiences and whale watching excursions bring you face-to-face with the region's marine life. The climate is temperate maritime—cooler and rainier than you might expect—so packing layers and waterproof gear is essential. Alaska delivers a cruising experience that is rugged, beautiful, and profoundly connected to nature in ways the Caribbean simply cannot match.

🚢 Top Ports in Alaska

Juneau

Mendenhall Glacier

Alaska's capital city sits at the base of the Gastineau Channel and is only accessible by boat or plane—no roads lead in or out. The star attraction is Mendenhall Glacier, a 13-mile river of ice flowing from the Juneau Icefield. The Mendenhall Visitor Center offers excellent ranger programs, and trails lead to glacier viewing points including the popular Photo Point and Nugget Falls. Beyond the glacier, Mount Roberts Tramway rises 1,800 feet above downtown for panoramic views, while the Alaska State Capitol building downtown offers free historical tours. Juneau's compact downtown has excellent seafood restaurants, particularly for fresh halibut and king crab.

Skagway

White Pass and Yukon Route Railway

This small Gold Rush town of just 1,000 residents transforms each summer when over a million cruise passengers pass through. The White Pass and Yukon Route Railway is the signature excursion—a narrow-gauge railway that climbs nearly 3,000 feet through 40 miles of spectacular mountain terrain, featuring 10 tunnels, trestle bridges, and views of the Brackett Wagon Road used by stampeders during the 1898 Gold Rush. Alternative excursions include the scenic drive to the Yukon border via the Klondike Highway, dog sledding on the Lynx Glacier, or kayaking in Taiya Inlet. Skagway's historic district features wooden boardwalks with Gold Rush-era buildings now housing shops and restaurants.

Ketchikan

Creek Street and Totem Bight State Historical Park

Known as the Salmon Capital of the World, Ketchikan sits at the southern entrance to Alaska's Inside Passage and receives an average of 150 inches of rain annually—so waterproof gear is essential. Creek Street, a historic boardwalk built on pilings over Ketchikan Creek, was once the town's red-light district and now houses shops and galleries. Nearby Totem Bight State Historical Park features a recreated Tlingit clan house and original totem poles, many restored by Civilian Conservation Corps workers in the 1930s-40s. The Great Alaskan Timberframe Company offers axe-throwing experiences and lumberjack shows, while fishing charters target king salmon, halibut, and steelhead.

Icy Strait Point

Whale watching and Hoonah culture

A private excursion destination owned by the Hoonah Tlingit, Icy Strait Point was developed specifically for cruise passengers seeking authentic Alaskan experiences. The main attraction is world-class whale watching in the nutrient-rich waters of Icy Strait—humpback whales congregate here in remarkable numbers during summer months. The historic salmon processing facility has been converted into a cultural center featuring Tlingit dance performances, artist demonstrations, and traditional food tastings. The longest zip line in North America (nearly 2 miles) soars above the forest canopy here, and brown bear viewing excursions operate in nearby streams during salmon season.

Sitka

St. Michael's Cathedral and Sitka National Historical Park

This former Russian capital preserves a unique legacy—the Russian-American Company established a settlement here in 1799, and St. Michael's Cathedral, built in 1844, remains the spiritual heart of Sitka's Russian Orthodox community. Sitka National Historical Park features a totem pole trail and the site of the 1804 Battle of Sitka between the Tlingit and Russians. The park's visitor center houses the oldest Russian artifacts in Alaska. Sitka is also renowned for wildlife—Steller sea lions haul out at local rookeries, sea otters populate the waters, and the area supports both brown and black bears. Deep-sea fishing charters target halibut weighing over 100 pounds.

🧳 What to Expect

☀️ Weather

Alaska's cruise season (May-September) features temperate maritime conditions very different from interior Alaskan extremes. Coastal temperatures range from 10-18°C in May, warming to 15-24°C in July-August before cooling in September. Rain is guaranteed—Ketchikan averages 150+ inches annually, while even 'drier' ports like Skagway see 60+ inches. Overcast skies are normal, not exceptional. Pack waterproof layers rather than heavy raincoats, and prepare for variable conditions within a single day. The Inside Passage can shift from brilliant sunshine to mist and drizzle within hours.

🍽️ Local Cuisine

Alaska is famous for its wild seafood—Pacific salmon (king, sockeye, coho), halibut, and king crab feature prominently on menus both onboard and ashore. In Juneau, Sample Alaska offers local fare with views of the channel. Skagway's Gold Rush Brewery serves solid pub food after a day of excursions. Ketchikan's salmon runs mean fresh-caught fish is ubiquitous. Ashore, expect higher prices than typical U.S. cities for meals—budget €25-45 per person for lunch at port restaurants. Onboard, Alaska-themed巡航 often feature salmon bakes, crab legs, and locally-sourced ingredients as part of your cruise fare.

🏛️ Culture & Customs

Alaska's indigenous peoples—the Tlingit, Haida, and Tsimshian in Southeast Alaska—have inhabited these lands for thousands of years, and their influence appears everywhere from totem poles to place names. Respect for indigenous culture is expected; ask before photographing tribal members or cultural ceremonies. Dress codes for Alaska cruises are casual throughout ships, though some specialty restaurants may require 'smart casual' dress. Excursions often involve rugged terrain—closed-toe shoes with good grip are essential for hiking and railway excursions. Gratuities follow U.S. norms (15-20%), and most services accept credit cards easily. Alaska's frontier culture values practicality and humor; don't be surprised by locals' directness or self-deprecating jokes about weather, mosquitoes, or the remoteness of their communities.

💰 Currency

U.S. Dollar (USD) is the sole currency used throughout Alaska. Euros are not accepted—ensure you have dollars or a credit card with no foreign transaction fees. Most ports have ATMs at the cruise terminal or within walking distance. Credit cards are universally accepted in shops, restaurants, and for excursion payments. When purchasing shore excursions through the cruise line, charges appear on your cabin account in USD regardless of your home currency. For small vendors or local crafts at markets, cash remains useful. Budget approximately $150-300 USD per person per day for meals ashore, souvenirs, and optional activities not covered by your cruise fare.

🗣️ Language

English is the universal language throughout Alaska's cruise ports and onboard all major cruise lines. You will have no language barriers in Juneau, Skagway, Ketchikan, or any other cruise destination. Native Alaskan languages—including Tlingit, Haida, and Tsimshian—are preserved in cultural centers and village communities, but visitors will interact in English. Cruise ships operating in Alaska are predominantly English-language vessels, though Princess and Holland America often carry a more international passenger mix and may offer multilingual daily programs on select sailings.

💡 Insider Tips

  • ★Book the earliest possible excursion time at Glacier Bay—ships typically arrive early morning when glaciers are clearest and most dramatic, before afternoon cloud cover builds. If your itinerary includes both Glacier Bay and Tracy Arm or Endicott Arm, save your glacier-hoping expectations for Glacier Bay; the smaller fjords often have too much debris to allow close approach.
  • ★The Alaska Railroad's Gold Star Dome cars between Anchorage and Denali offer scenery competitive with any cruise excursion—and often at a fraction of the cost. A one-way journey costs approximately $150-200 USD versus $300+ for comparable shore excursions, with glass-dome ceilings providing panoramic views of Denali, the Alaska Range, and wildlife including moose and caribou.
  • ★Princess Cruises and Holland America both offer 'EXC Tours' (Excellence Collection Tours) at premium pricing, but their standard Adventure Tours often provide identical itineraries at 20-30% lower cost. Norwegian Cruise Line's 'Free at Sea' promotions can include complimentary shore excursions when booked in a package—check current promotions carefully before booking individual tours.
  • ★Ketchikan's free attractions—including walking the historic Creek Street boardwalk, watching the salmon leap at Ketchikan Creek during peak season (July-August), and browsing the免税 shops along the waterfront—can fill several hours without spending a dime. Save your excursion budget for Icy Strait Point's zip line or whale watching, which are genuinely premium experiences worth the investment.

🎒 Packing Tips

  • ✓Waterproof jacket with hood and rain pants—rain is virtually guaranteed, especially in Ketchikan, and cotton clothing becomes uncomfortable when wet
  • ✓Layering pieces including fleece or wool mid-layer and quick-dry base layers—temperatures fluctuate between sunny deck time and air-conditioned interiors throughout each day
  • ✓Sturdy closed-toe shoes with good traction—wet wooden boardwalks, railroad ties, and mountain trails require proper footwear; hiking boots ideal for Denali extensions
  • ✓Polarized sunglasses and binoculars—essential for wildlife spotting (whales, eagles, bears) and glacier viewing from distance; reduces glare off water and ice
  • ✓Compression layers and hand warmers for glacier excursions—standing on deck watching glaciers can be cold even in summer; temps at glacier level feel significantly colder
  • ✓Daypack or small backpack—useful for carrying layers, snacks, camera gear, and purchased items while exploring ports
  • ✓Universal power adapter if needed—U.S. uses Type A/B outlets (two flat pins); European cruisers should bring adapters for charging at the port
  • ✓Motion sickness remedies—Inside Passage crossings can be choppy; bring wristbands, medication, or ginger supplements as a precaution

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best time of year to cruise Alaska?▼
Late May through mid-August offers the optimal Alaska cruise experience. June provides the longest days (18-19 hours of sunlight), peak wildlife activity, and the best chance of calving glaciers. July and August are slightly warmer but rainier—Ketchikan receives over 12 inches of rain in August alone. Late September sees reduced daylight (12-13 hours), cooling temperatures, and many attractions closing, though early-season May departures can offer shoulder pricing with acceptable conditions if you accept variable glacier accessibility.
Do I need a visa for Alaska cruises?▼
Most Alaska cruises departing from U.S. ports (Seattle, Whittier, Seward) require only a valid passport for your nationality—no visas for standard tourism. However, itineraries visiting Victoria, British Columbia require all passengers to clear Canadian immigration; non-visa-required nationalities (including EU citizens) need only an ETA (Electronic Travel Authorization) for Canada, obtainable online in minutes for CAD $7. American citizens need only proof of citizenship (passport or enhanced driver's license). Any passenger who has visited certain countries may require a U.S. visa regardless of destination. Always verify with your cruise line 60+ days before departure.
How many days do I need in Alaska?▼
A 7-night Inside Passage cruise provides an excellent introduction, visiting 3-4 ports and featuring at least one glacier or fjord experience. However, Alaska's scale is vast—many cruisers feel rushed. 10-14 night voyages allowing one-way sailings (Vancouver to Whittier or reverse) dramatically improve the experience with less port-hopping repetition and opportunities for ports like Sitka, Petersburg, and Haines. We strongly recommend adding 2-3 nights pre or post-cruise: either a Denali National Park land extension (accessible via Alaska Railroad from Anchorage or Whittier) or a stay in either Vancouver or Seattle to explore beyond the port.
What should I budget for excursions in Alaska?▼
Alaska shore excursions range from free (self-exploration of ports, hiking trails, downtown shopping) to premium experiences exceeding €400 per person. Standard excursion categories: Adventure activities (zip-lining, dog sledding, kayak tours) typically €120-200; Wildlife/whale watching tours €80-150; Historical/cultural tours €60-120; Train excursions (White Pass Railway, Alaska Railroad) €150-250 for half-day, €300+ for full-day. We recommend budgeting €200-400 per person for 2-3 signature excursions and enjoying the ports independently for additional activities. Booking through the cruise line offers convenience and guarantee of ship departure, but independent operators at port often provide comparable experiences at 15-30% lower cost.

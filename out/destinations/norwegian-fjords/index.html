About Norwegian Fjords

The Norwegian Fjords represent one of Europe's most dramatic and awe-inspiring cruising destinations, where towering glacier-carved mountains plunge into deep, glassy waters surrounded by cascading waterfalls and picturesque villages. A Norwegian Fjords cruise delivers an intimate encounter with nature that large-ship ocean cruising simply cannot match—you'll sail past the UNESCO-listed Geirangerfjord with its famous Seven Sisters waterfall, through the narrow Nærøyfjord where cliffs rise 1,700 metres above your ship, and into remote corners where Viking history and modern Scandinavian culture blend seamlessly. Whether you're departing from Southampton, Copenhagen, or Hamburg, the journey into these ancient fjords offers breathtaking scenery at every turn, with opportunities to ride the legendary Flåm Railway, hike to Preikestolen (the Pulpit Rock), or simply soak in the midnight sun during summer months. This destination suits cruisers seeking natural beauty, outdoor adventure, and authentic Scandinavian experiences with the comfort of ship-based accommodation.

🚢 Top Ports in Norwegian Fjords

Flåm

Flåm Railway & Nærøyfjord

The tiny village of Flåm is the gateway to one of the world's greatest railway journeys. The Flåm Railway descends 864 metres over 20 kilometres with a 1-in-18 gradient through 20 tunnels, offering views of the Rjoande waterfall accessible directly from the train. Combine with a ferry through the Nærøyfjord, a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the narrowest fjords in Europe, where cliffs rise dramatically on either side of your boat.

Geiranger

UNESCO World Heritage Fjord

Geirangerfjord is the crown jewel of Norwegian cruising, a 15-kilometre fjord flanked by towering mountains and spectacular waterfalls. The Seven Sisters waterfall plunges 250 metres on the southern side while the Suitor waterfall faces it on the northern cliff. Excursions include the Eagle's Road with its 11 hairpin bends and spectacular viewpoints, or the Stryn summer ski centre for year-round snow.

Bergen

Bryggen Wharf & Fløibanen Funicular

Norway's second-largest city and often the starting or ending point of fjord itineraries, Bergen offers a perfect blend of history and nature. The UNESCO-listed Bryggen harbour features colourful wooden warehouses dating to the Hanseatic era, while the Fløibanen funicular whisks you to Mount Fløyen for panoramic city and fjord views. Don't miss the fish market for fresh seafood including the famous Bergen fjord shrimp.

Olden

Briksdal Glacier & Loen Skylift

Nestled at the end of the Nordfjord, Olden provides access to the spectacular Briksdal Glacier, a glacial tongue extending from the Jostedalsbreen icecap. Glacier walks and amphibious boat tours offer close encounters with this ancient ice formation. For thrills, the Loen Skylift cable car climbs 1,011 metres to Mount Hoven in just five minutes, offering breathtaking views over Lake Lovatnet.

Ålesund

Art Nouveau Architecture

Ålesund is famous for its unique Art Nouveau architecture, rebuilt in the Jugendstil after a devastating fire in 1904. The town centre features ornamental turrets, towers, and decorative facades that make it one of Norway's most photogenic cities. Excursions include the Aksla viewing platform (accessible by 418 steps or the funicular) for panoramic views, and the Atlantic Sea Park, one of Northern Europe's largest salt-water aquariums.

🧳 What to Expect

☀️ Weather

Norwegian Fjord cruising operates primarily between May and September. June temperatures average 12-18°C in the lowlands though fjord walls create microclimates that can be significantly cooler. Rainfall is possible in any month—pack layers and waterproofs regardless of the season. The famous midnight sun appears north of the Arctic Circle from late May to mid-July, creating extraordinary golden light for late-evening deck viewing. Interior fjords can be markedly warmer than the open coast.

🍽️ Local Cuisine

Norwegian cuisine centres on fresh seafood, game meats, and dairy. Must-try dishes include fjord salmon (Norwegian salmon is world-renowned), gravlaks (cured salmon with dill), reindeer steaks, and lutefisk (traditional dried cod). In Bergen, seek out the fish market for fresh prawns and crab. Most fjord villages have small supermarkets where you can buy Norwegian brown cheese (gastrointestinal), rugbrød (dense rye bread), and waffles with sour cream and jam. Cruise ship menus typically blend international cuisine with Norwegian specialities on fjord-themed nights.

🏛️ Culture & Customs

Norwegians value punctuality, personal space, and environmental responsibility. Tipping is not obligatory in restaurants or for taxis—service charges are typically included. Dress is casual throughout the cruise, but for onshore excursions consider layers and sturdy walking shoes; Norway's terrain can be rugged. In summer, locals embrace outdoor culture with hiking and cycling, so activewear is entirely appropriate. Norwegians are reserved but friendly and welcoming to visitors.

💰 Currency

Norway uses the Norwegian Krone (NOK), NOT the Euro. While some tourist shops in major ports accept euros or credit cards, you will need NOK for smaller establishments, markets, and some rural areas. ATMs are readily available in all cruise ports. Credit and debit cards are universally accepted almost everywhere, even for small purchases. Inform your bank before travelling to avoid card blocks.

🗣️ Language

Norwegian (Bokmål and Nynorsk) is the official language, but English is spoken fluently by virtually everyone, from hotel staff to fishermen. All cruise excursions, menus, and signage in tourist areas are available in English. You will rarely encounter a language barrier in any Norwegian port.

💡 Insider Tips

  • ★Book the Flåm Railway as a self-guided excursion rather than through the cruise line to save 30-40%. The train runs regularly and you can simply purchase tickets at Flåm station upon arrival—your cruise ship docks steps away from the station.
  • ★The secret to photographing the Seven Sisters waterfall in Geiranger is to book a morning arrival when the sun is behind you. Afternoon cruise ships often get the waterfall in shadow with the sun in front.
  • ★The Bergen Card (available at the tourist office near the fish market) gives free public transport, discounts at museums, and reduced prices on the Fløibanen funicular—it pays for itself if you're in Bergen for more than a few hours.

🎒 Packing Tips

  • ✓Waterproof jacket with hood—fjord weather changes rapidly and you'll want to be on deck for scenic sailing even during light rain
  • ✓Layers including merino wool or synthetic base layers—temperatures vary between the warm ship interior, cool tender boats, and potentially cold outdoor viewing points
  • ✓Hiking boots or sturdy walking shoes with good grip—the famous Norway walks involve uneven terrain, wet paths, and steep sections
  • ✓Polarised sunglasses—reducing glare off the water makes a significant difference when photographing fjord scenery from the deck
  • ✓Compact binoculars—essential for spotting waterfalls in the distance, nesting eagles, and wildlife on mountain slopes inaccessible from shore
  • ✓Power adapter for European/C-Type sockets—standard on most cruise ships operating in Europe
  • ✓Small daypack for shore excursions—carrying water, snacks, and layers comfortably makes exploring easier

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best time of year to cruise Norwegian Fjords?▼
June offers the optimum combination of long daylight hours (up to 19 hours), maximum fjord access, and the famous Norwegian strawberry season. The Flåm strawberry festival occurs around midsummer. July and August provide more stable weather and school holiday schedules but larger crowds and higher prices. September offers spectacular autumn colours, the cod fishing season, and lower prices with fewer passengers—though days shorten to around 13-14 hours.
Do I need a visa for Norwegian Fjords cruises?▼
UK citizens don't need a visa for Norway as it's part of the Schengen Area. EU citizens need only their national identity card. US, Canadian, and Australian citizens can visit Norway for up to 90 days without a visa under the Schengen agreement. If your itinerary includes Russia (some expedition cruises) or other non-Schengen countries, check specific requirements. Always confirm with your cruise line as visa policies vary by nationality and itinerary.
How many days do I need in Norwegian Fjords?▼
A minimum of 7 nights provides a satisfying introduction covering 3-4 fjord ports (typically Bergen, Flåm, Geiranger, and one additional port). This allows 1-2 days at sea for scenic cruising and 4-5 port days. For serious exploration including the North Cape, Hardangerfjord, or overnight stays, 10-14 nights are ideal. Consider adding 2-3 land days in Bergen or Oslo pre or post-cruise to visit the Bryggen museum, Mount Fløyen, and connect with the Norway in a Nutshell train journey.
What should I budget for excursions in Norwegian Fjords?▼
Excursion prices in Norwegian Fjords typically range from €45-75 for standard tours (flåm Railway, city walking tours, bus transfers) to €120-200 for premium experiences (glacier walks, kayaking, helicopter flights). A full-day Norway in a Nutshell combined ticket costs approximately €85. Budget approximately €200-350 per person for 2-3 excursions over a 7-night cruise. Free activities include hiking trails from most ports, swimming in the fjord (bracing even in summer), exploring Bryggen in Bergen, and admiring waterfalls from the ship. Many fjord villages are compact enough to explore independently on foot in an hour or two.

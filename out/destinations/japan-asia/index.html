About Japan & Asia

Japan & Asia represents one of cruising's most rewarding destinations, where ancient temples coexist with neon-lit megacities and centuries-old traditions meet cutting-edge innovation. A cruise through this region typically departs from Tokyo, Shanghai, Hong Kong, or Singapore, weaving between bustling port cities that offer extraordinary contrasts at every stop. The culture shock is part of the adventure—expect to encounter immaculate public spaces, culture (queueing), and a profound respect for order and cleanliness that makes every port a pleasure to explore. This destination delivers a holiday unlike anywhere else: you're not just visiting countries but crossing worlds, from the serene bamboo groves of Kyoto to the futuristic skyline of Shenzhen.

🚢 Top Ports in Japan & Asia

Tokyo (Yokohama)

Ultra-modern metropolis meets historic Japan

Japan's capital city is a mandatory port featuring the iconic Shibuya Crossing, Senso-ji Temple in Asakusa, the upscale shopping of Ginza, and the pop-culture wonderland of Akihabara. Most ships dock in Yokohama, just 30 minutes from central Tokyo by train. Don't miss the Tokyo Skytree for panoramic views or a traditional tea ceremony experience.

Kyoto (via Osaka/Kobe)

Ancient capital of traditional Japan

The cultural heart of Japan, Kyoto offers over 2,000 temples and shrines including the golden Kinkaku-ji, the vermillion gates of Fushimi Inari, and the bamboo groves of Arashiyama. The Gion district provides chances to spot geishas. Most cruise lines tender from Kobe or dock in Osaka—this port alone justifies the entire cruise.

Hiroshima

Powerful history and nearby Miyajima Island

A port of profound significance, Hiroshima's Peace Memorial Park and Atomic Bomb Dome offer moving reflection on WWII history. Just a 30-minute ferry ride away, Miyajima Island features the iconic floating torii gate of Itsukushima Shrine, ranked among Japan's top three scenic views.

Hong Kong

World-class dining, shopping, and Victoria Peak

One of Asia's most vibrant ports, Hong Kong combines British colonial heritage with Cantonese dynamism. Take the Peak Tram to Victoria Peak for unparalleled city views, explore the floating seafood restaurants of Aberdeen, or shop at the sprawling Temple Street Night Market. The Star Ferry crossing of Victoria Harbour is quintessentially Hong Kong.

Busan

South Korea's maritime gem

Korea's second-largest city offers stunning beaches, the massive Haedong Yonggungsa cliff-side temple, and Gamcheon Village with its colorful stacked houses. The Jagalchi Fish Market lets you pick your seafood, then have it cooked on-site. Busan's Haeundae Beach and the relaxing Jeju Island day-trips are major draws.

Shanghai

China's gleaming financial powerhouse

A city of superlatives—home to the world's fastest maglev train and the world's second-tallest building, the Shanghai Tower. The Bund waterfront showcases colonial architecture, while the Old City reveals traditional gardens and the iconic Yu Garden. Nanjing Road offers world-class shopping, and the French Concession provides European-style café culture.

Taipei (Keelung)

Night markets, temples, and Taipei 101

Taiwan's capital delivers incredible street food culture—Shilin Night Market is legendary for bubble tea, oyster omelets, and stinky tofu. Taipei 101 was once the world's tallest building and offers observation deck views. The National Palace Museum houses 700,000 Chinese imperial artifacts, while the ornate Longshan Temple provides spiritual immersion.

🧳 What to Expect

☀️ Weather

March-April brings 10-18°C with cherry blossoms and occasional rain. May warms to 18-25°C. October-November offers 12-22°C with autumn foliage. Summer months (June-September) see 25-32°C with 80% humidity and typhoon risk. Carry a light rain layer year-round and layer clothing—temples can be cool inside despite warm outdoor temperatures.

🍽️ Local Cuisine

Japanese cuisine dominates: sushi, sashimi, ramen, tempura, okonomiyaki, and kaiseki (multi-course haute cuisine). Hiroshima is famous for okonomiyaki (savory pancake). Taiwan offers night market favorites like bubble tea, beef noodle soup, and oyster vermicelli. Korean ports feature bibimbap, Korean BBQ, and fresh seafood. Shanghai serves Cantonese dim sum, xiaolongbao (soup dumplings), and regional Shanghainese cuisine. Cruisers should try kaiseki if possible—it's an affordable luxury in Japan at ~€30-50 per person.

🏛️ Culture & Customs

Remove shoes when entering temples, traditional restaurants, and some homes (look for shoe racks at entrances). Bowing is customary for greetings—return any bow you receive. Speaking loudly on public transport is frowned upon. Carry small bills for temple offerings and vending machines. Tipping is not expected and can be considered rude in Japan. Photography at temples is usually permitted but check for restrictions. During cherry blossom season, locals practice (hanami) —picnicking under the trees—this is a beautiful cultural moment to observe or join.

💰 Currency

Japan uses Japanese Yen (JPY) — €1 ≈ ¥165. China uses Chinese Yuan (CNY) — €1 ≈ ¥7.8. South Korea uses Korean Won (KRW) — €1 ≈ ¥1,450. Taiwan uses Taiwan Dollar (TWD) — €1 ≈ ¥3.8. Credit cards are accepted at major hotels and department stores in Japan, but cash remains essential for small restaurants, temples, and local shops. Notify your bank before travel. ATMs at 7-Eleven (Japan) and FamilyMart accept foreign cards. Euro-denominated shore excursions can be booked through the cruise line for convenience.

🗣️ Language

Japanese is the primary language in Japan; English signs exist in major tourist areas but basic Japanese phrases help immensely—learn ありがとうございます (arigatou gozaimasu) for thank you. Mandarin Chinese predominates in mainland China and Taiwan. Korean is spoken in South Korea. Tourist-facing staff at major attractions generally speak some English. Download Google Translate with offline Japanese and Mandarin language packs—essential for menus, signage, and basic communication. A translation app literally saved many cruise passengers from ordering something unexpected.

💡 Insider Tips

  • ★Book Kyoto day tours through the cruise line's optional excursions, but insist on the later departure from Osaka/Kobe. You'll arrive before the tourist crowds and have 30 extra minutes at major sites. Alternative: take the ship-organized shuttle to Osaka station, then the ¥890 JR Special Rapid train directly to Kyoto station (45 minutes)—faster than organized tours and saves €50-80 per person.
  • ★Visit convenience stores (7-Eleven, FamilyMart, Lawson) for affordable, excellent meals. Japan's convenience store food rivals restaurant quality—try onigiri (rice balls), bento boxes, fried chicken (karage), and fresh salads. Breakfast set meals cost €3-5 and are far better than cruise ship breakfast buffets.
  • ★Purchase a prepaid Suica or ICOCA card at any Japanese train station for ¥2,000 (including ¥500 deposit). This rechargeable card works on all subways, buses, and convenience store purchases. Download the Suica app if you have an iPhone with NFC—refill it instantly without visiting a machine. Saves enormous time fumbling for tickets.
  • ★In Hong Kong, use the MTR subway instead of taxis—it's faster, cheaper, and air-conditioned. Get an Octopus Card at any MTR station for ¥150 (¥50 refundable). Download the Citymapper app for accurate public transit directions in all major Asian cities—far more reliable than Google Maps in this region.

🎒 Packing Tips

  • ✓Comfortable walking shoes with rubber soles — essential for temple steps and city cobblestones
  • ✓Light rain jacket or packable poncho — sudden showers common year-round
  • ✓Modest clothing for temple visits — cover shoulders and knees at religious sites (sarongs/lightweight pants pack small)
  • ✓Narita/Keisei train pass — book online before arrival for Japanese airport access
  • ✓Universal power adapter — Japanese outlets accept Type A/B plugs (same as US); European Type C adaptors won't work
  • ✓Small crossbody bag or money belt — pickpocketing is rare but secures valuables in crowded markets
  • ✓Japanese phrasebook or offline translation app — essential for menus, shopping, and basic interactions
  • ✓Reusable water bottle with filter — tap water is safe; filtered bottles reduce plastic waste
  • ✓Long-sleeved layers — temples require covered shoulders; air-conditioned venues get cold
  • ✓Swimwear — onsen (hot spring) ports like Beppu or Hakone offer onsen experiences at some excursion stops

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best time of year to cruise Japan & Asia?▼
Late March to early May offers the iconic cherry blossom season with temperatures of 12-20°C—prices peak during Golden Week (late April to early May). October-November delivers autumn foliage with similar temperatures and fewer crowds. Avoid June through September due to typhoon season (15-20 named storms annually) and oppressive humidity reaching 85%. January-February offers the fewest tourists and lowest prices but cold temperatures (3-12°C) and limited cherry blossom views. For first-time visitors, April combines manageable crowds, excellent weather, and sakura season.
Do I need a visa for Japan & Asia cruises?▼
Japan offers visa-free entry for most Western passport holders (90 days). South Korea grants visa-free access for 90 days. Taiwan allows 90 days visa-free for EU, UK, US, Canadian, and Australian passport holders. China requires a visa for most nationalities—your cruise line typically handles visa documentation for included ports, but verify this before booking. If your itinerary includes Shanghai or Beijing as independent extensions, you'll need a separate Chinese tourist visa (L visa, ~€130, processing 4-10 business days). Hong Kong and Macau permit 90-180 days visa-free for most Western nationalities. Always check your specific passport and itinerary.
How many days do I need in Japan & Asia?▼
A minimum 7 nights captures 3-4 ports effectively—ideal for first-time visitors focusing on Japan. However, 10-14 nights allows deeper exploration including South Korea, Taiwan, and multiple Japanese cities. Most cruisers regret rushing through; add 2-3 pre- or post-cruise hotel nights in Tokyo or Hong Kong to fully experience these world-class cities. Tokyo alone warrants 3-4 days ( Tsukiji outer market, Shibuya, Akihabara, day trips to Nikko or Kamakura). If you want both Tokyo and Kyoto properly, consider a 14-night itinerary or a land-sea combination.
What should I budget for excursions in Japan & Asia?▼
Ship-organized shore excursions typically range €75-250 per person. Budget options: Self-guided city exploration using public transit costs €5-15 for unlimited daily subway passes. Mid-range experiences (food tours, cooking classes, cultural workshops) cost €45-95 through companies like Urban Adventures or local tour operators booked in advance. Premium experiences (private guides, tea ceremony with geisha, kaiseki lunch, golf at exclusive courses) run €150-400+. For a 10-night cruise visiting 6 ports, budget €400-800 per person for 3-4 excursions, plus €200-400 for self-guided days. Always book popular Kyoto and Tokyo excursions early—these sell out first.
Is Japan safe for solo cruisers and female travelers?▼
Japan consistently ranks among the world's safest countries. Violent crime is extremely rare, even late at night. Solo female travelers report feeling comfortable using public transport and walking alone. However, maintain standard precautions: watch your belongings in crowded tourist areas (occasional pickpocketing), and be aware that language barriers can create isolation. Joining ship-organized excursions provides social opportunities and local context. Japanese society is generally reserved; expect politeness rather than outgoing friendliness from strangers. The safety factor is a major reason Japan & Asia appeals to experienced solo cruisers.

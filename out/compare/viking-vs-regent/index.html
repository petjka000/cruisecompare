Viking Cruises vs Regent Seven Seas 2026

An honest, detailed comparison to help you choose the right cruise line.

Compare > Viking Cruises vs Regent Seven Seas 2026

Viking Cruises and Regent Seven Seas represent two distinct approaches to premium ocean cruising. Viking operates a fleet of identical, purpose-built ships with a strict adults-only policy and destination-focused itineraries. Regent Seven Seas offers all-inclusive luxury with spacious suites, complimentary shore excursions, and a more traditional upscale cruise experience. Both target affluent travellers seeking enrichment over entertainment, yet differ fundamentally in pricing structure and inclusions. Viking appeals to culturally curious adults valuing Scandinavian design and educational programming, while Regent attracts those wanting seamless, all-inclusive indulgence with generous space ratios and personalised service.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which is better value?▼
Value depends on consumption patterns. Viking's lower base fare (€2,840 vs €4,850 for seven nights) suits moderate drinkers and independent excursion planners. Regent's comprehensive inclusion—unlimited premium beverages, all excursions, gratuities, and intercontinental business-class airfare—delivers superior value for travellers who would otherwise purchase these separately. Calculate your typical onboard spending: if exceeding €1,500 per person weekly, Regent likely prevails. Viking's transparent pricing rewards self-disciplined travellers, while Regent eliminates decision fatigue and surprise bills. Both offer early booking incentives; Viking frequently includes airfare promotions that narrow the gap considerably.
Which for families?▼
Regent Seven Seas accepts children of all ages; Viking enforces a strict 18-plus policy with no exceptions. Families must choose Regent, though with important caveats. Regent provides no dedicated children's facilities, youth counsellors, or age-appropriate programming—unlike Crystal, Seabourn, or expedition lines. Children are welcome but largely self-entertained. Family suites are limited and expensive. For multi-generational groups where adults prioritise luxury and children are mature, self-sufficient travellers, Regent functions adequately. Families requiring structured children's activities should consider mainstream premium lines instead. Viking's adults-only policy creates intentional tranquillity unsuitable for family travel.
How do prices compare?▼
Viking's entry-level seven-night Mediterranean sailing averages €2,840 inside or €3,650 veranda; comparable Regent itineraries start at €4,850 for the smallest suite with no inside options. Regent's fare includes business-class intercontinental flights (€1,200-2,000 value), unlimited shore excursions (€150-400 daily value), and premium beverages throughout. Viking includes beer, wine and soft drinks with meals only; spirits cost extra. Shore excursions run €89-299 each. When normalising for inclusions, Regent's effective premium narrows to 30-40% rather than headline 70%. Both lines offer significant early booking discounts, loyalty benefits, and occasional promotions including Viking's popular 2-for-1 airfare deals.
Which has better food?▼
Both excel, but differently. Regent's Culinary Arts Kitchen, Le Cordon Bleu partnership, and speciality venues including Chartreuse (French) and Pacific Rim (Asian) demonstrate culinary ambition. Suite guests enjoy in-suite dining from any restaurant menu. Viking's Manfredi's Italian and Chef's Table tasting menus earn consistent praise, with no surcharges anywhere. Viking emphasises regional destination cuisine and local market sourcing. Regent offers greater variety and formal gastronomy; Viking provides consistent quality with simpler, Nordic-influenced elegance. Regent's room service and suite dining options surpass Viking's more limited in-cabin offerings. Food-focused travellers slightly favour Regent; destination-dining enthusiasts prefer Viking.
Which for first-timers?▼
Viking suits curious, independent first-timers seeking structured cultural immersion without cruise clichés. The adults-only environment eliminates complexity, while included excursions and transparent pricing reduce decision anxiety. Scandinavian design feels accessible rather than intimidating. Regent's all-inclusive model removes all financial friction—ideal for first-timers fearing surprise bills—though the luxury environment and older demographic may feel imposing. Viking's slightly larger ships (930 vs 490-750 guests) offer more social opportunities for nervous solo travellers. First-timers prioritising relaxation and pampering should choose Regent; those wanting educational engagement and contemporary atmosphere prefer Viking. Both avoid the overwhelming scale of mass-market lines.
Main differences?▼
Fundamentally, Viking is premium destination-focused cruising for adults; Regent is ultra-luxury all-inclusive hospitality. Viking prohibits children, emphasises enrichment lectures and documentary programming, and maintains Scandinavian efficiency with moderate space ratios. Regent welcomes all ages, prioritises spacious suites and personalised service, and includes virtually everything upfront. Viking's nine identical ships enable consistent, predictable experiences; Regent's four vessels vary in age and character. Viking's included thermal spa and Nordic bathing culture contrast Regent's traditional spa and casino. Dining philosophies differ: Viking's regional, educational approach versus Regent's classical luxury gastronomy. Ultimately, Viking attracts intellectually curious travellers; Regent appeals to indulgence-seeking connoisseurs.

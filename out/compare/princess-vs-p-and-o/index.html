Princess Cruises vs P&O Cruises 2026

An honest, detailed comparison to help you choose the right cruise line.

Compare > Princess Cruises vs P&O Cruises 2026

Princess Cruises and P&O Cruises represent two distinct approaches to premium cruising under the Carnival Corporation umbrella. Princess delivers international sophistication with strong North American and Australasian routes, while P&O serves British tastes with home-port convenience from Southampton. Both occupy the mid-market segment but diverge in atmosphere, dining philosophy, and target demographics. Princess emphasises destination immersion and modern ship design, whereas P&O prioritises traditional cruising values, formal nights, and UK-centric convenience. Understanding these nuances helps travellers select the line matching their holiday expectations and travel style preferences for 2026 sailings.

Verdict by Traveller Type

FamiliesPrincess Cruises

Frequently Asked Questions

Which is better value?▼
P&O Cruises typically delivers superior headline value for British travellers. Their fares include gratuities (saving €105-126 per week), use sterling currency avoiding exchange rate fluctuations, and drinks packages cost €10 less daily. Princess appears competitive until adding €15-18 daily service charges, USD conversion costs, and higher specialty dining. However, Princess includes more in their base fare—better production shows, Medallion technology, and often superior itinerary port times. For transparent budgeting, P&O wins. For inclusive quality experiences, Princess may justify the premium. Compare total voyage costs including all anticipated extras rather than brochure prices alone.
Which for families?▼
Princess Cruises excels for families with children across their entire fleet. Princess Pelicans divides into three age groups (3-7, 8-12, 13-17) with dedicated facilities, while teen spaces remain open late. Critically, P&O operates two adult-only mega-ships (Arvia and Iona), immediately eliminating them for family consideration. P&O's family-friendly Britannia and Ventura offer competent kids' clubs but lack Princess's consistency. Princess Medallion technology helps parents locate children and message cabinmates. Family shore excursions and Discovery at SEA programmes add educational value. For guaranteed family suitability and superior children's facilities, Princess is the reliable choice.
How do prices compare?▼
P&O's €699 seven-night inside average undercuts Princess at €799, but direct comparison misleads. P&O includes gratuities worth €105-126; Princess adds these separately. P&O's sterling pricing eliminates 2-3% card foreign transaction fees. Drinks packages diverge €10 daily. Factor these and P&O's effective advantage grows to €200-250 weekly. Balcony cabins show narrower gaps—Princess €1,199 versus P&O €1,099. Princess commands premiums for newer Sun Princess, Alaska routes, and exotic itineraries. P&O dominates value for Mediterranean and Canary Islands from Southampton. Always calculate total anticipated spend including beverages, WiFi, excursions, and onboard spending rather than comparing cabin rates alone.
Which has better food?▼
Princess leads in specialty dining diversity and international cuisine execution. Their partnership with Chef Curtis Stone elevates SHARE restaurant, while Sabatini's Italian and Crown Grill steakhouse consistently impress. Main dining rooms offer solid international standards with regional destination menus. P&O counters with distinctly British appeal—superior afternoon tea, curry nights responding to passenger demand, and comfort food execution that resonates with domestic palates. P&O's main dining rooms occasionally disappoint with mass-catering inconsistencies, though their Sindhu (Atul Kochhar) and Epicurean restaurants match Princess specialty quality. Food preference ultimately divides along cosmopolitan versus traditional British tastes rather than objective quality hierarchy.
Which for first-timers?▼
P&O Cruises suits British first-timers seeking familiar comforts—sterling currency, included gratuities, Southampton departures eliminating airport stress, and entertainment featuring recognisable personalities. The structured environment with fixed dining options reduces decision fatigue. However, Princess better introduces cruisers to international cruising standards—superior service consistency, more diverse fellow passengers, and technology smoothing embarkation and onboard navigation. Princess's OceanMedallion removes friction first-timers often find daunting. For nervous travellers wanting minimal surprises, P&O. For those seeking to understand why cruising captivates globally, Princess provides the more representative introduction. Both exceed expectations for debut cruisers compared to budget alternatives.
Main differences?▼
Atmosphere fundamentally diverges: Princess feels internationally cosmopolitan, P&O unmistakably British. Princess employs USD, adds gratuities, and embraces flexible dining; P&O uses GBP, includes tips, and preserves traditional fixed seating with formal nights. Technology separates them dramatically—Princess's Medallion enables seamless service, P&O retains conventional cruise cards. Entertainment reflects origins: Princess partners with Discovery and Broadway producers; P&O books British TV stars and prioritises ballroom dancing. Ship deployment matters—P&O's adult-only Arvia and Iona exclude families entirely, while Princess maintains family suitability fleet-wide. Finally, P&O's Southampton dominance versus Princess's multi-home-port model shapes itinerary accessibility and flight requirements for British travellers.
